Research

Agriculture

Climate-smart management decisions have the ability to improve agriculture’s adaptive capacity and potentially mitigate climate change impacts. We aim to inform agricultural and decision support models through analyzing historical climate variability, developing future climate scenarios, and exploring the relationship between agricultural systems and local climate.

Northeast Precipitation

The Northeastern United States has experienced a substantial increase in heavy precipitation over the last century. We aim to characterize the temporal and spatial attributes of this increase and investigate the climatological mechanisms that have led to increased heavy precipitation.

Crop modeling

Crop models can be used to quantify plant water requirements, predict the impact of water shortages on yield, and calculate water productivity. However, current algorithms are not well suited to water limited areas. We are developing a new framework to simulate growth stage based deficit irrigation under limited seasonal water availability.

Teaching

The global agricultural sector faces the significant challenge of feeding a population projected to increase to 9 billion by mid-century under an evolving climate. This course will explore the physical geography of agricultural production systems throughout the world with an emphasis on the interactions between crops, climate, water, soils, and technology.

Climate is a fundamental driver of populations, economies, and cultures. Over the past century, humans have been modifying the atmosphere through the emission of greenhouse gasses. This course will provide an overview of the Earth’s climate system and the physical basis, impacts, and societal dimensions of climate change.

Humans have radically altered the distribution of water on Earth. We’ve built cities in deserts supplied with water from hundreds of miles away, extracted enough groundwater to alter the Earth’s gravitational field, and dammed sixty-five percent of global freshwater flows. This course will: 1) Introduce students to the physical geography of water, 2) Survey human interactions with water through case studies from around the world, and 3) Explore how climate change and population growth will affect future quality and availability of water.

Jonathan Winter is an Assistant Professor in the Department of Geography and Adjunct Assistant Professor in the Department of Earth Sciences at Dartmouth College. Before moving to the Upper Valley in the summer of 2013, Jonathan was an Associate Research Scientist at Columbia University Earth Institute and a NASA Postdoctoral Program Fellow at Goddard Institute for Space Studies in New York City. Jonathan received his PhD from Massachusetts Institute of Technology and completed his undergraduate studies at SUNY College of Environmental Science and Forestry in Syracuse, NY. Outside of academia, Jonathan spends his time traveling, hiking, exploring VT/NH with his family (wife Teresa, sons Lucas, Nolan, and Henry, dog Taz), snowboarding, and trying to find the latest and greatest food and music. Trevor Partridge is a PhD student in the Department of Earth Sciences., co-advised with Erich Osterberg. Academically he is interested in the relationship between agriculture and the climate. He anticipates using regional climate models to broaden our understanding of how agricultural practices are impacting the climate, while also working to improve farmer’s adaptive capacity for future impacts. Prior to coming to Dartmouth, Trevor earned a MS in Horticulture from Cornell University and a BS in Geology from Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute in Troy, NY. When not in the office, you’ll find Trevor hiking, camping, or generally exploring VT and NH with his dog Winifred, cooking, or attempting to bake bread.

Charlotte Cockburn is an MS student in the Department of Earth Sciences. She is interested in the causes and impacts of extreme weather in the Northeastern United States. Her research uses regional climate modeling to predict future climate change in this region. Before landing in New Hampshire, Charlotte earned a BS in Earth System Science from McGill University in Montreal. Charlotte also worked in McGill’s Global HydroLAB, studying the causes and impacts of river intermittency at a global scale. Outside of work, Charlotte enjoys skiing, embroidery, hiking, and making very lopsided vases in the ceramics studio.

Natalie Teale is a postdoctoral fellow with the Dartmouth Society of Fellows. Her research interests are centered on precipitation variability and its related impacts. She anticipates investigating the hydroclimatic variables associated with streamflow in the northeastern US with the goal of understanding how climate change affects high-impact hydrologic events. She earned a PhD in Geography from Rutgers University, a MS in Geography from Texas A&M University, and a BA in Earth Sciences and Geography from Syracuse University. Prior to moving to New Hampshire, she taught in the Geography Department at the University of Tennessee, Knoxville. Outside of academia, Natalie enjoys gardening, hiking, and exploring the regional scenery.

Samuel Purdom is a PhD student in the EEES Program. His research will focus on using crop and climate models to investigate the impact of climate change on crop production. Samuel holds an MS in Plant Science from University of Tennessee Knoxville, a BS in Environmental Resource Management from Virginia Tech, and is a returned Peace Corps Volunteer from Zambia. In his free time, Samuel loves whitewater kayaking, skiing and biking, making pizza, playing banjo, learning new languages, and obtaining as much fresh air as possible.
